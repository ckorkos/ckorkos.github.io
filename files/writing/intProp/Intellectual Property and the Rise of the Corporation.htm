Intellectual Property and the Rise of the Corporation

By Tu Luu, Jaynee Lee, and Chris Korkos - An examination of the laws protecting intellectual property in the context of a digital age, and the changing relationship between the individual and the corporation.

  1. Copyright was established in America by the Copyright Act of 1790, which granted creative ownership to “promote the progress of science and useful arts” (Donner). The act promoted learning among authors, artists, and inventors by securing “the sole right and liberty of printing, reprinting, publishing and vending” (Donner) of each work for fourteen years. While it began by protecting an individual's ideas, copyright has more recently been used by powerful corporations at the expense of underrepresented individuals. The growth of Internet and increased distribution of media has further blurred the line separating theft from art.
  2. The History of Copyright
  3. Since its establishment, the life of a copyright has grown from fourteen years to more than ninety-five years, and in some cases far longer than the author’s life. In 1998, the Digital Millennium Copyright Act criminalized some cases of copyright infringement. The radical changes prompt a question: who is benefiting from the current, far more extreme intellectual property policies?

  5. The major push from the original length of copyright can be attributed to Walt Disney’s famous and profitable Mickey Mouse cartoon. For years Disney benefitted from the character, but when their ownership was threatened by a copyright expiration in 1984 the company lobbied Congress for support. “In 1976, Congress authorized a major overhaul of the copyright system assuring Disney extended protection” (Schlackman). The "Mickey Mouse Curve" can be seen in the attached graph, which compares the growth of copyright duration with the time that Disney has protected its intellectual property. Note that Disney’s IP includes creative materials that it has bought, in addition to original content.

  7. This demonstrates a perversion of copyright’s original purpose: “to promote the progress of science and useful arts” (Donner). When global, billion-dollar companies sue artists over intellectual property which the companies didn't necessarily create, the laws aren't ensuring progress of art or expression. Instead they are monopolizing entertainment by controlling profitable content and ensuring power over potential opponents.
  8. Copyright with Advancing Technology
  9. Copyright laws have become unclear and hazy with the pace at which technology is advancing. It is becoming increasingly difficult to regulate copyright laws with the doors that technology has been opening. The culture the world lives in today incorporates so many practices that may come in conflict with the subject of copyright. It can be hard to determine whether copyrights have been violated or if copyright laws need to change in accordance with the drastic changes of technology.
  10. Advancing technology has created a number of factors that directly impact copyright laws. Internet simplified the redistribution of digitized information. Reproducing the information is inexpensive to do and there is no loss in quality compared to the original. Those copies can also be easily duplicated and distributed to millions of people instantly. The Internet allows us to be extremely well connected that someone in America could send information to people all over the world with just a few clicks.

  12. These days, the terms of copyright laws are so vague and unclear that many people do not realize they are actually breaking the law. Reusing other people's songs, images, and ideas is so common that many forget that what they are doing is illegal. YouTube star Michele Phan was victim to an infringement lawsuit for millions of dollars. She had unintentionally violated copyrights for a few songs by using them in her YouTube videos without proper permission. Michelle's actions are not uncommon - the hazy lines of copyright laws cause many people to unintentionally commit internet plagiarism and leave them subject to unexpected lawsuits.
  13. Copyright for Capitalists?
  14. With the increase in copyright laws that support capitalist ideals, there comes a bottleneck in innovation. Today’s copyright term lengths are playing a big role in the increase of the wealth gap in America. With the copyright law passed in 1978 known as the Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act or Mickey Mouse Protection Act “… automatically protects a work that is created and fixed in a tangible medium of expression on or after January 1, 1978, from the moment of its creation and gives it a term lasting for the author’s life plus an additional 70 years [...] For works made for hire and anonymous and pseudonymous works, the duration of copyright is 95 years from first publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter…” (Copyright Basic).
  15. This enabled larger companies to make money from an idea for many years. After securing a popular copyright, companies no longer care for newer creations but rather milk the existing IP. This contributed to companies such as Warner Music (owners of “Happy Birthday”), Disney and other entertainment conglomerates forming significant monopolies. Wealthier companies and individuals also have better access to lawyers, which gives them power over opponents. Copyright laws originally motivated creators by giving recognition and a living wage, but in today’s society, it has turned into a corporate scheme for wealth and power. An example of one of many corporate schemes that feeds off their copyright ownership is copyright trolling.
  16. Sarah Jacobson's “So You’re Being Sued for Piracy” defines copyright trolling as “threatening, scaring, and embarrassing people into paying a neat sum without ever actually going to court.” Sarah further explains “Copyright trolls don't want to go to court. Doing so is costly and risky--neither of those things is good from the standpoint of a copyright troll. It's much cheaper and easier for them if people who receive letters simply pay up, instead of forcing an actual court case.”

  18. Copyright trolling is not limited to internet piracy. These companies and freelancers prey on those who literally can’t afford to fight back. They use their ownership to force out innovation, favoring wealth over change.
  19. As the wealth gap continues to increase in the US, it is necessary to advocate for creative equality. To begin fighting for ways to adjust copyright laws to meet today's lifestyles of growing technology and dropping economy. To work towards a more fair and just set of copyright laws that do not allow long term monopolies.
